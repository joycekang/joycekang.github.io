<blockquote>
	Stem-cell differentiation is the process by which a more specialised cell is formed from a stem cell, 
	leading to loss of some of the stem cell's developmental potential.
	—Nature
</blockquote>

One thing I've been thinking about a lot lately is this idea of differentiation, of specializing
at the expense of losing stem-like potential. As a first-year medical student, I was pretty 
undifferentiated. We would get to hear lectures from experts in everything from immunology to 
endocrinology, and it was easy to become interested in *literally everything*. Similarly, as we 
rotate through various labs in search of a PhD thesis lab, it's difficult to go from a smorgasbord 
of potential research topics and environments to selecting one lab working on one particular subfield. 
Looking forward into second year and especially into PhD-land, one hard realization to embrace is 
that its impossible to be an expert in everything, and we must eventually choose to specialize. 

Why is specializing so hard, I mean emotionally hard? I think part of it is that the MD/PhD phenotype
is in part one of indecisiveness and keeping options open: we basically signed up for double 
the years of training because we couldn't commit to doing either degree alone. Differentiating is 
also scary because it feels like once you walk through one door, other doors that were once open 
become closed. At our recent HST retreat, we asked Dean Hundert for advice about this. He told us, 
"You are allowed to mourn the closing of those doors." At first, it may seem a bit arrogant to mourn 
the closing of career doors you never even embarked on, but it's okay to mourn the lost *potential*.

He also told us about this idea of doors in space and doors in time, which I found to be quite 
helpful. The gist is this: there are doors in *space*, which are opportunities available to you
if you go out and seek them, like setting up a meeting with a PI or joining a club. There
are also doors in *time*, which are doors that may open transiently and remain open for a 
short while, but you must be awake and looking for them in order not to miss them. You cannot
predict what doors in time will open for you, but when they do, it's important to be prepared 
and be courageous enough to potentially walk through them at the expense of the path you are 
currently on. I felt like that was wise career advice for not only medical and graduate students 
making major decisions about specialization but also young adults of all professions 
navigating the post-grad career landscape. 

As a young trainee, it's easy to feel like choosing a research topic or area to specialize in 
is somehow binding or will determine your entire career trajectory. I find it comforting to
periodically remind myself that much of one's career and life is a random walk, and many
opportunities and choices await that you cannot even predict right now. What we can do in the 
moment is simply make the most immediate decision and make the most of whatever path we are 
currently on. And as long as we are for the most part happy in the day-to-day of it, I'd 
consider that a success.
